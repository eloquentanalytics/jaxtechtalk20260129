These aren't obstacles to overcome. They're the boundaries of the playing field.

EXISTING CUSTOMERS

  • LinkedIn: Recruiters pay for premium, job posts, InMail credits. If LinkedIn launched AI recruiting, they'd be competing directly with the people who pay them $10K+/year for Recruiter licenses.
  • Intuit: Accountants are a huge referral channel for QuickBooks. In 2024, Intuit ran an ad campaign suggesting "you might not need an accountant" - massive backlash from partner accountants who felt betrayed after decades of partnership. Now trying to rebuild trust.
  • Shopify: Merchants are the entire business. Unlike Amazon, Shopify can't sell products directly to consumers - they'd be competing with every store on their platform. This is actually their competitive advantage vs Amazon.
  • Salesforce: AppExchange has 7,000+ apps from partners. If Salesforce built competing features, the ecosystem would collapse. They have to be very careful about what they build vs. what they leave to partners.
  • Stripe: Can't compete with the businesses using their payment processing. Their "platform risk" docs explicitly address how they won't compete with customers.

COUNTER-EXAMPLE: What happens when you DO compete with customers

  • Amazon: Actively competes with its own merchants via AmazonBasics. Merchants are fleeing to Shopify because Amazon copies their best-selling products and undercuts them. This is why "Shopify vs Amazon" is framed as "merchant-first vs consumer-first."
  • Apple App Store: Built Screen Time (competed with parental control apps), built Notes/Reminders (competed with productivity apps). Developers constantly fear being "Sherlocked."

CURRENT REVENUE

  • Kodak: Invented the digital camera in 1975, shelved it to protect film business. Filed bankruptcy 2012.
  • Nokia: Had touchscreen prototypes in 2004, but Symbian licensing was too profitable. Went from 50% to 3% smartphone share by 2013.
  • Newspapers: Print ads were 80%+ of revenue. Couldn't pivot to digital fast enough - lost $40B in ad revenue 2000-2015.
  • Blockbuster: Passed on buying Netflix for $50M in 2000. Late fees alone were $800M/year.
  • Cable companies: Knew streaming was coming, but couldn't unbundle $100/month packages.
  • BlackBerry: Physical keyboard was the brand. Couldn't abandon it even as iPhone dominated.

COUNTER-EXAMPLES (Companies that DID self-cannibalize)

  • Apple: Killed the iPod with iPhone. Killed iPhone sales with bigger iPads. Keeps cannibalizing itself.
  • Netflix: Killed their own DVD business to go streaming before the tech was even ready.
  • Amazon: Launched Prime Video knowing it would hurt DVD/Blu-ray sales.

CHANNEL CONFLICTS

  • Nike: When they pushed DTC too hard, Foot Locker reduced Nike shelf space. Had to find balance.
  • Microsoft: 95% of enterprise sales go through partners. Can't bypass them without destroying the channel.
  • Samsung: Needs Best Buy for displays, demos, returns. Can't undercut them on Samsung.com.
  • Adidas: Online exclusives angered retail partners who invested in inventory and floor space.
  • Apple: Even with Apple Stores, still needs Best Buy, carriers for reach. Strict MAP pricing to keep peace.
  • Cisco: Entire business built on VAR (value-added reseller) channel. Going direct would collapse the model.

Key insight: The problem isn't seeing the disruption coming - most incumbents DO see it. The problem is that their current revenue makes it economically irrational to respond.

Navigating the Labyrinth

The Finance Problem

CapEx rules: Must capitalize software > $X

OpEx pressure: "Temporary" has no budget line

Decommission fantasy: Old systems don't turn off

Dual-running costs hidden in different budgets

The Vibe Code Opportunity

Greenfield only: No legacy dependencies

New segments: Customers with no existing systems

Internal tools: Where ugly is acceptable

Fast, cheap, experimental ‚Äî and disposable

Vibe coding is made for throwaway stuff. Enterprise finance isn't.

Navigating the Labyrinth

Enterprise

Budget the writeoff: bridges are infrastructure

Vibe code the greenfield: new segments, internal tools

Easier: Markets with deep legacy (banking, healthcare)

Easier: Long sales cycles ‚Äî you iterate faster

Harder: Cloud-native incumbents

Harder: Tight regulatory review of changes

The startup is born in the upside down. The enterprise has to find a door.

Enterprise

The Startup Playbook

1 PLANS Leverage natural defenses
2 PREPARATION Specialize to gain an edge
3 LEARNING Learning is dirty work
4 SPOILS Pursue a retreating enemy

Lack of resources is a strength when new markets appear, not a weakness.

The Enterprise Playbook

1 PLANS Grow in ways that don't threaten structural constraints
2 PREPARATION Find ONE process that's 100% automatable ‚Üí beachhead
3 LEARNING Find projects that can be vibe coded ‚Üí AI-native from start
4 SPOILS Direct AI within growing units ‚Üí they reinvest the gains

Don't fight the structure. Work within it ‚Äî or around it.
