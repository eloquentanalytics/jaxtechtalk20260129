Enterprises are hemmed in by their current structures in three key ways. These aren't obstacles to overcome with enough willpower ‚Äî they're the boundaries of the playing field. The default enterprise approach is lots of small pilots. A complex enterprise has a variety of processes serving different customer groups. Each has differing levels of automation opportunity. The problem: AI is extremely challenging to extrapolate from the demo to the pilot to the rollout. What works in a controlled demo often fails at scale. So enterprises scatter their bets across many pilots, hoping something sticks.

3 Navigating the Labyrinth

The middle layer that shouldn't exist

"This won the 2005 DARPA Challenge. Ugly. Necessary."

  • Legacy systems expose UIs, not APIs ‚Äî AI needs APIs
  • You need a middle layer for handoffs ‚Äî it doesn't exist
  • The throwaway bridge becomes load-bearing before you notice
This is Stanley's trunk ‚Äî the car that won the 2005 DARPA Grand Challenge. A mess of wires, duct tape, and improvisation. This is what AI transformation actually looks like in the middle. The core problem: your existing systems were built for humans clicking through workflows. They expose UIs, not APIs. AI needs structured, API-friendly interfaces. New AI systems aren't built for humans either. So you need something in the middle ‚Äî a handoff layer that satisfies both. That handoff layer doesn't exist. You build it. And here's the trap: building it is a significant engineering project with no direct revenue. Ownership is unclear ‚Äî is this an AI project or an IT project? Budget battles ensue. The "throwaway" code you vibe code to bridge them gets promoted to production without proper engineering. It becomes load-bearing before anyone notices. The handoff layer becomes permanent technical debt that slows all future changes.
3 Navigating the Labyrinth

Why enterprises can't build throwaway code

The Finance Problem

CapEx rules: Must capitalize software > $X

OpEx pressure: "Temporary" has no budget line

Decommission fantasy: Finance assumes old systems turn off. They don't.

Dual-running costs hidden in different budgets

The Vibe Code Opportunity

Greenfield only: No legacy dependencies

New segments: Customers with no existing systems

Internal tools: Where ugly is acceptable

Fast, cheap, experimental ‚Äî and disposable

Vibe coding is made for throwaway stuff.
Enterprise finance isn't.

Here's why enterprises struggle with the ugly middle: corporate finance rules. CapEx rules require capitalizing software projects over a threshold ‚Äî which means documentation, approval chains, depreciation schedules. But vibe-coded bridges are "temporary." There's no budget line for temporary. OpEx pressure means every dollar spent needs ongoing justification. Technical debt budgets don't exist ‚Äî "refactoring is never funded because the system works." The decommission fantasy: every business case assumes old systems will be turned off and savings will materialize. In practice, edge cases require legacy to remain. Data migration is incomplete. Legacy becomes "system of record" by accident. No one is accountable for shutdown ‚Äî it's everyone's problem. Result: dual-running costs are hidden across different budgets. The promised savings never materialize. The opportunity for enterprises: find the projects that CAN be vibe coded. Greenfield. New customer segments. Internal tools where ugly is acceptable. That's where you can move fast.
3 Navigating the Labyrinth

Navigating the ugly transition

Enterprise

Go microservices: smaller pieces depreciate faster, more can be reused

Budget the writeoff: bridges are infrastructure, not projects

Vibe code the greenfield: new segments, internal tools

Name the decommission owner with hard sunset dates

Startup

Easier: Markets where incumbents have deep legacy (banking, healthcare, logistics)

Easier: Industries with long sales cycles ‚Äî you iterate faster

Harder: Markets where incumbents are already cloud-native

Harder: Industries with tight regulatory review of changes

The startup is born in the upside down.
The enterprise has to find a door.

For enterprises: GO MICROSERVICES: Corporate finance rules about depreciation and writeoffs work in your favor if you break things into smaller pieces. A monolith has to be fully deprecated over 5-7 years. Microservices can be swapped out piece by piece ‚Äî smaller writeoffs, faster depreciation, and more components can be reused in the new architecture. BUDGET THE WRITEOFF: Accept that bridges become permanent. Budget for them as infrastructure, not projects. The business case should assume dual-running costs, not decommissioning savings. This is depreciation on an asset, not a failed project. VIBE CODE THE GREENFIELD: Find projects that CAN be vibe coded ‚Äî new customer segments, internal tools, new markets. These can be AI-native from the start without legacy integration headaches. NAME THE DECOMMISSION OWNER: If you DO plan to decommission, name a person accountable for shutdown. Set hard sunset dates. Accept some functionality loss to avoid dual systems forever. For startups ‚Äî where the upside down is easier vs harder: EASIER markets: - Where incumbents have deep legacy systems (banking, healthcare, logistics, insurance). They're stuck with decades of technical debt. You're not. - Industries with long enterprise sales cycles. While they're doing 18-month procurement, you're iterating weekly. By the time they choose a vendor, you've shipped 50 versions. HARDER markets: - Where incumbents are already cloud-native (newer SaaS companies, digital-first businesses). They can move almost as fast as you. - Highly regulated industries with slow approval processes for any changes. Your iteration speed advantage disappears if every change needs 6 months of compliance review. The key insight: your advantage isn't just that you're starting fresh ‚Äî it's that you're starting fresh in markets where THEY can't start fresh.

The Startup Playbook

Turn every barrier into your advantage

1 CHOOSING THE PATH Map their constraints ‚Üí build exactly there
2 PREPARING 100% of a narrow slice ‚Üí serve the "$50K floor"
3 NAVIGATING AI-native from day one ‚Üí no legacy to integrate
4 RESISTING Follow the layoffs ‚Üí build where they're cutting

You have nothing to sacrifice.
That's not a weakness. That's the moat.

For startups, every barrier becomes an advantage: SCOPE: Don't try to automate 80% of everything. Automate 100% of one thing. Find the "$50K floor" in your industry ‚Äî the customers incumbents rationally ignore because the economics don't work at their cost structure. UPSIDE DOWN: You don't have legacy systems to integrate with. Build AI-native from day one. While enterprises are building ugly bridges between old and new, you're building the future. SHORTEST PATH: When enterprises announce layoffs, they're telling you where they've decided not to compete. Follow the layoffs. Build AI-native solutions for the customers they're abandoning. SACRIFICE: Map their constraints explicitly. Who are their sacred customers? What revenue can't they risk? What channels are they locked into? Build exactly there. They structurally cannot follow you. Example: Recruiting under $50K. Recruiters won't touch jobs below $50K (commission math doesn't work). Build an AI recruiting service that ONLY serves that market at 1/10th the price. They can't respond ‚Äî margin compression would destroy their valuation.

The Enterprise Playbook

Navigate the barriers ‚Äî don't pretend they don't exist

1 CHOOSING THE PATH Grow in ways that don't threaten structural constraints
2 PREPARING Find ONE process that's 100% automatable ‚Üí beachhead
3 NAVIGATING Find projects that can be vibe coded ‚Üí AI-native from start
4 RESISTING Direct AI within growing units ‚Üí they reinvest the gains

Don't fight the structure.
Work within it ‚Äî or around it.

For enterprises, the barriers are real. Don't pretend they don't exist. Navigate them: SCOPE: Stop scattershotting pilots across everything. Find the ONE process that can be 100% automated. Make it your beachhead. Prove the model there, then expand to adjacent processes. UPSIDE DOWN: Find projects that can be vibe coded ‚Äî greenfield, no legacy dependencies, AI-native from day one. New customer segments, new internal tools, new markets. That's where you can move like a startup. SHORTEST PATH: Direct AI investments within growing units ‚Äî they're more likely to reinvest the gains into accelerating growth rather than just cutting costs. Don't direct AI to dying units hoping to save them. SACRIFICE: Grow in ways that don't threaten your structural constraints. Don't compete with your customers. Don't cannibalize your core revenue. Don't disrupt your channels. Find the negative space where you can grow without triggering any of those structural tripwires. The danger: cutting your way to irrelevance while startups eat your future. The opportunity: finding the pockets of freedom within your constraints and moving fast there.
